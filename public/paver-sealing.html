After 30 years sealing South Florida pavers, marble, travertine, and more, we've learned there are no shortcuts to doing it right. Sealing isn't a one-afternoon job — it's a careful, multi-day process, and every step matters. Here's exactly how we do it.

What we seal

We seal most outdoor hardscape surfaces — pavers, marble, travertine, and similar stone and tile. (We can seal concrete as well, though it's a less common request.) If you're not sure whether your surface can be sealed, just ask — after three decades we've seen just about everything South Florida has.

Our process, step by step

Clean, sand, seal — each with its own day and proper dry time. Weather-dependent from start to finish.

Day One — Deep Clean

Everything starts with a thorough deep cleaning. For pavers especially, that means working the dirt out from between every joint and removing the weeds growing in them — not just rinsing the surface. We use a cleaning solution that's tough on grime but gentle on your surrounding grass and plants. Once the surface is clean, we go back over any stubborn weeds until they're virtually all gone, and blow off any loose leaves and debris.

Then it needs to dry. We give the surface a full day before moving on — and the whole process is weather-dependent from here, so we plan around South Florida's rain.

Right after cleaning, we put up caution ribbon and stakes on driveways. We don't want anyone — including you — driving on the freshly cleaned surface and leaving tire or dirt marks before we've finished. Protecting the clean is part of the job. (Patios don't get taped, since there's no vehicle traffic.)

Day Two — Sand the Joints

When the job calls for it, we fill the joints with polymeric jointing sand — a material that hardens in place to lock the joints and help suppress weeds and ants. (Not every job needs it; some surfaces we go straight to sealing.)

We sweep the sand into every gap by hand with a broom, taking our time so each joint fills completely and none is left sitting on the paver faces. A light pass with a blower clears the loose material off the tops — gently, so it stays packed in the joints. Then we mist the whole surface with water several times so the sand sets, binds, and locks in. Done right, it looks clean and uniform, with no empty joints. Then it dries another day.

Day Three — Seal

Finally, the sealant — applied by hand with rollers, one or two of our crew depending on the size of the job. We choose the right sealant for the conditions: a water-based sealant during the rainy season, and a premium solvent-based sealant when the weather's dry or in the winter months. The reason matters — choosing correctly for the forecast protects against hazing or whitening if rain comes before the sealant cures. After 30 years we know exactly which to use and when.

Both options bring out the rich, deep color of your pavers and add a protective layer that helps your surface last for years instead of months.

After We Seal

The caution ribbon and stakes stay up for about 48 hours after sealing. Car weight can leave grooves or tire marks in sealant that hasn't fully cured, so it's important to keep vehicles off. Light foot traffic is lower-risk, but we recommend keeping off it as much as possible. After about 48 hours, your surface is cured, ready to use, and done — clean, color-restored, sanded, and sealed.

Paver Sealing FAQ

Can you seal without cleaning first?

No — that's the one rule of sealing. The pavers have to be deep-cleaned and fully dry before the sealant goes on. Seal over dirty pavers and you lock the dirt, algae, and old joint sand in. That's why we always do it as one project: clean first, dry, sand, then seal.

How long does sealing last in South Florida?

Years rather than months for most properties. Heavy sun and a lot of car traffic shorten it; shaded back patios and pool decks hold longer. We'll tell you straight at the next visit whether it's still holding or it's time for a refresh.

What is the polymeric joint sand for?

The sand between pavers is structural — it locks each paver against its neighbors so the surface doesn't shift, and it helps suppress weeds and ants in the joints. Polymeric sand hardens in place when it sets, so it stays put and looks clean and uniform.

Do you seal pool decks, patios, and walkways too?

Yes — same service, same multi-day process. Pool decks and patios are usually a bit more shaded, so they tend to hold longer. Walkways and entrance pavers we treat like driveways since they take the foot traffic.
